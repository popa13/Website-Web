Seminars -- Mathematical Horizons

Since the Winter 2026 semester, a mathematics seminar has been established at the Université du Québec à Trois-Rivières. The objective of this series is to introduce undergraduate mathematics students to various research topics in an accessible manner. For instance, the first presentation focused on summability theory and its application to calculating the value of divergent series. The presentations are recorded and can be watched on the seminars' YouTube channel.

If you know any professors or students who are passionate about their research topic and would like to share their work with a broader audience, please do not hesitate to contact me by email at pierre-olivier.parise@uqtr.ca. 04-23 Philippe-André Luneau UL Acceleration of the Fixed-Point Method via a Reduced-Order Model

Abstract : The fixed-point method is a widely used numerical method for solving various nonlinear equations, including discretized partial differential equations (PDEs). One can even develop schemes to apply the fixed-point method to systems of such equations. In this setting, solving a large number of linear systems is required, which becomes computationally expensive. Dimensionality reduction techniques can therefore be employed to lower the computational cost. In this talk, we will examine how the error arising from the approximate resolution of one of the equations propagates through the fixed-point iterations when solving a system of PDEs. This will lead us to study the relationships between the sensitivity of complex composed function structures and certain classical combinatorial objects. Rigorous error estimates on the final solution of this iterative process will be derived. An application in fluid mechanics with thermal convection will be presented to demonstrate the effectiveness of the methodology.
04-09 Oussama Ben Nasr UQTR Law of Quadratic Reciprocity and Application to Blum's Protocol

Abstract : This talk is dedicated to the law of quadratic reciprocity, for which I will present a proof and some applications to quadratic congruences. I will then briefly illustrate these results through Blum's 'coin flipping by telephone' protocol, in order to show their connection to cryptography.

03-26 Mathieu Cloutier UQTR Hunting caterpillars in an aperiodic environment

Abstract : In a given universe where the most advantaged animals are those with the most fingers relative to their size, what would these animals look like? We ask this question in the universe of Penrose P2 tilings, a family of aperiodic tilings, where animals are connected and acyclic sets of tiles and where their fingers are the tiles of degree 1. We then resolve several conjectures proposed by C. Porrier, A. Goupil, and A. Blondin Massé. We show that the animals that maximize the number of fingers relative to their size are all caterpillars (in the graph-theoretic sense), with at most one appendage of at most 6 tiles. We also investigate the problem of bi-infinite caterpillars: we disprove the conjecture that there is only one such caterpillar.

03-12 Jean-Philippe Burelle UdS Cohomology et impossible shapes

Abstract : Cohomology theories are present in virtually every branch of mathematics. They are algebraic objects that allow us to measure various notions of non-triviality of an object. I will introduce cohomology through impossible geometric figures. In this case, the cohomology associated with a planar figure measures, in a certain sense, whether that figure can be the projection of a genuine three-dimensional object. This idea comes from a 1992 paper by the mathematician-physicist Roger Penrose.

02-26 Benjamin Gauthier UQTR Generating Maximal Snake Polyominoes Using Deep Neural Networks

Abstract : As part of a research project during the summer of 2025, we explored the use of artificial intelligence to support mathematical research. Specifically, we sought to contribute to the resolution of a complex problem: the generation of maximal snake polyominoes, which are the longest possible chains of cells within a grid. Today, the generation of these mathematical objects is limited due to algorithmic and computational complexity, which becomes insurmountable in large grids. To this end, we tested two deep neural network models: a Transformer model, which generates snakes cell by cell, and a diffusion model, which generates a snake by learning to remove noise from an image. This presentation describes the functioning of these models as well as our primary results.

02-12 Yan Lanciault UQAM Christoffel inclusion : a new characterization of Christoffel words

Abstract : As classical objects in combinatorics on words, Christoffel words offer a wide range of surprising interpretations that intertwine through equivalent definitions, each providing the opportunity to bridge mathematical subjects that are sometimes distant from one another. More than 15 equivalent definitions have been proposed since the study of these words began. Two new definitions have been introduced over the last two years right here in Quebec. Christoffel inclusion is the most recent among them.
02-05 Guillaume Brouillette UQTR Your tshirts have three holes : An introduction to homotopy

Abstract : Intuitively, a sweater appears to have four openings: two for the sleeves, one for the neck, and one for the waist. However, from a topological point of view, it can be shown that it only has three! This presentation will explain how homotopy theory leads to this conclusion by studying shapes through continuous deformations. The concepts of homotopy and homotopy equivalence will be presented both formally and through several visual examples. Subsequently, cell complexes (CW complexes) will be used as a natural framework to construct (and deconstruct) spaces from elementary cells. Finally, these tools will allow us to simplify various topological spaces, including a model of a sweater. This talk assumes no prior knowledge of topology.

01-22 Pierre-Olivier Parisé UQTR An excursion in Summability Theory

Abstract : Infinity in mathematics brings its share of paradoxes when not handled properly. As far back as Antiquity, Zeno of Elea raised doubts regarding the treatment of an infinite sequence of operations by formulating his famous paradox: will an arrow ever reach its target if it must always cover half of the remaining distance? Today, thanks to modern definitions of series convergence—notably those of Bolzano-Weierstrass and Augustin Cauchy—we know that the arrow does indeed reach its target. Nevertheless, some series are divergent, yet certain mathematicians manage to assign them a value. In this presentation, I will discuss number series and certain paradoxes related to the values assigned to specific divergent series. More precisely, after a historical review of the early stages of series theory, I will present a summation method developed by Ernesto Cesàro, which allows for the rigorous assignment of a value to certain divergent series. Finally, I will introduce a theory specifically developed for handling divergent series, known as summability theory.
